## Questions

1. PFAS stands for: A) primary filtration and sedimentation B) public fluoride assessment standard C) pressure-flow adjustment system D) per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances
2. PFAS are often called forever chemicals because they are generally: A) only found in chlorine B) persistent and resistant to breakdown C) always radioactive D) living organisms
3. PFOA stands for: A) perfluorooctanoic acid B) primary fluoride organic analyte C) polyfilter oxidation agent D) potable finished ozone acid
4. PFOS stands for: A) perfluorooctane sulfonic acid B) primary fluoride ozone scale C) public finished organic solids D) pressure filtration operating standard
5. A common historical source category for PFAS contamination is: A) firefighting foam and industrial uses B) natural limestone only C) chlorine gas only D) filter sand only
6. PFAS may also be associated with: A) only ozone feed B) only copper pipe C) only volcanic rock D) stain-resistant, nonstick, waterproof, and grease-resistant products
7. A common analytical approach for select PFAS in drinking water is: A) settleometer only B) pH paper only C) LC-MS/MS methods such as EPA Method 533 or 537.1 D) visual color wheel only
8. In drinking-water PFAS reporting, ppt is commonly equivalent to: A) mg/L B) psi C) ng/L D) gpm
9. 4 ppt is equivalent to: A) 4 mg/L B) 4 gpm C) 4 ng/L D) 4 NTU
10. 1 µg/L equals: A) 100 ng/L B) 1 ng/L C) 1,000 ng/L D) 10 ng/L
11. EPA's 2024 final PFAS NPDWR listed PFOA and PFOS enforceable MCLs as: A) 0.080 mg/L each B) 1 NTU each C) 4 ppt each D) 10 mg/L each
12. EPA's 2024 final rule used a Hazard Index concept for certain PFAS mixtures involving: A) nitrate and nitrite only B) Giardia and viruses only C) PFHxS, PFNA, HFPO-DA/GenX, and PFBS D) lead and copper only
13. A Maximum Contaminant Level Goal is: A) a required sample bottle size B) always the same as a public notice deadline C) a pump speed D) non-enforceable health-based goal
14. An MCL is: A) only a lab method B) legally enforceable contaminant limit C) only a recommended taste level D) a pipe pressure target
15. California notification levels are best understood as: A) health-based advisory levels for chemicals without state MCLs B) pump alarms C) lab invoices D) federal permits only
16. A California response level generally indicates: A) a customer billing class B) a chlorine contact basin volume C) a level where source removal, treatment, blending, or public notification may be recommended/required depending on rule context D) a filter backwash rate
17. If PFAS are detected above an applicable response level, the operator/system should: A) ignore because PFAS are invisible B) flush one hydrant and close the case C) increase fluoride only D) follow DDW direction, evaluate source use/treatment/blending, and handle required notification
18. The best operator response to a PFAS detection is: A) change unrelated pump bearings B) assume lab error without review C) verify data quality, identify affected source/sample point, coordinate with compliance staff/regulator, and plan response D) hide the result
19. PFAS sampling requires special care because: A) pH strips are enough B) samples can be collected in any food container C) cross-contamination from common materials can affect low-level results D) chlorine residual destroys PFAS immediately
20. Examples of PFAS sampling contamination sources can include: A) only stainless steel pipes B) certain waterproof clothing, cosmetics, food wrappers, or PTFE-containing materials C) only turbidity standards D) only glass bottles
21. PFAS treatment commonly includes: A) granular activated carbon, ion exchange, and reverse osmosis/nanofiltration B) sedimentation alone C) standard chlorination alone D) fluoridation alone
22. GAC removes many PFAS primarily by: A) gravity settling B) converting them to chlorine C) adsorption D) UV color change
23. Ion exchange removes PFAS by: A) exchange/sorption on engineered resin B) boiling water C) hydrant flushing only D) pH paper
24. Reverse osmosis removes PFAS by: A) corrosion coupons B) pressure-driven membrane separation C) floc blanket settling only D) chlorine demand
25. A key operational concern for GAC PFAS treatment is: A) fluoride color B) hydrant caps C) media breakthrough and replacement/regeneration planning D) tank altitude only
26. A key operational concern for ion exchange PFAS treatment is: A) jar-test paddles only B) resin exhaustion, selectivity, regeneration/disposal issues C) chlorine gas pressure D) finished-water temperature only
27. A key operational concern for RO PFAS treatment is: A) backflow testing only B) pH meter color C) filter-to-waste only D) concentrate disposal and membrane management
28. Standard chlorine disinfection is generally: A) enough to remove all forever chemicals B) not considered a primary PFAS removal method C) the best PFAS destruction method in distribution D) required to convert PFAS to lead
29. Blending may reduce delivered concentration but: A) destroys PFAS chemically B) does not destroy or remove the contaminant mass C) works without compliance review D) eliminates monitoring need
30. Source substitution can be useful when: A) no regulator coordination is needed B) another approved source has lower contaminant levels and capacity is available C) all sources have identical chemistry D) the contaminated source is the only source
31. A T5-level PFAS decision should consider: A) only pump color B) only taste C) only daily production target D) public health, compliance, source capacity, treatment feasibility, residuals, waste streams, and communication
32. PFAS compounds differ from each other in: A) chain length, functional group, mobility, treatability, and toxicity data B) only color C) only spelling D) only pH
33. Short-chain PFAS can be harder for some adsorptive media because they may be: A) more mobile and less strongly adsorbed B) destroyed by visible light C) easier to settle by gravity D) converted to turbidity
34. PFHxS is: A) a pipe valve B) a turbidity method C) a PFAS compound D) a bacterium
35. PFNA is: A) a pump bearing B) a PFAS compound C) nitrate as nitrogen D) a disinfectant residual
36. HFPO-DA / GenX is: A) a replacement PFAS associated with fluoropolymer manufacturing B) a storage tank coating inspection only C) a filter media size D) a coliform test
37. PFBS is: A) a shorter-chain PFAS included in certain mixture/hazard-index discussions B) a chlorine residual C) a pressure unit D) a bacteriological sample type
38. Microplastics are generally defined as: A) dissolved oxygen B) chlorine residual C) nitrate ion D) small plastic particles, often discussed from 5 mm down to very small particles
39. Nanoplastics are generally: A) equivalent to PFOS B) larger than gravel C) smaller plastic particles below the microplastic range D) always visible to the eye
40. Microplastics are a real issue because they are: A) measurable and under active health/method-development research B) removed only by chlorine C) imaginary and untestable D) created only by operators
41. A key microplastics challenge is: A) lack of one simple standardized routine method for all particle sizes and polymers B) no environmental presence C) immediate acute MCL exceedance in all systems D) impossibility of any lab observation
42. Treatment processes that may reduce particles, including some microplastics, can include: A) pressure gauges only B) billing software C) coagulation, sedimentation, filtration, membrane processes D) fluoride only
43. Microplastics should be discussed carefully because: A) they are the same as Giardia B) they are not particles C) the science is real but risk, methods, and regulation are still developing D) they are already fully regulated like nitrate everywhere
44. Cyanotoxins are associated with: A) cross-connection assemblies B) harmful algal blooms/cyanobacteria C) copper service lines only D) pump cavitation
45. Common cyanotoxins of drinking-water concern include: A) PFOA and PFOS only B) microcystins, cylindrospermopsin, anatoxin-a, and saxitoxin C) lead and copper only D) hardness and alkalinity only
46. Cyanotoxin response may require: A) source monitoring, treatment optimization, public-health coordination, and communication B) stopping samples C) ignoring reservoir blooms D) lowering all coagulant
47. 1,4-dioxane is commonly considered: A) a pressure unit B) a disinfectant residual C) an emerging/unregulated contaminant of concern in some contexts D) a filter loading rate
48. Perchlorate is important because it affects: A) tank level B) pump vibration C) turbidity calibration only D) thyroid-related health concerns and has drinking-water regulatory history
49. An operator should describe emerging contaminants as: A) contaminants with evolving occurrence, health, method, or regulatory status B) always treated by chlorine C) fake contaminants D) always unmeasurable
50. The most exam-safe attitude toward PFAS and emerging contaminants is: A) treat all as identical B) memorize social-media claims C) know current regulated/advisory status, detection methods, likely sources, treatment options, and communication duties D) assume no monitoring applies
