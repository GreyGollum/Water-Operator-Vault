## Questions

1. Raw water turbidity rises rapidly after a storm. The operator should first: A) increase awareness, verify process response, and adjust treatment based on data B) ignore it C) disable turbidity alarms D) stop all records
2. Finished water turbidity exceeds an internal alarm limit. The best first response is: A) delete trend B) lower coagulant blindly C) assume sensor failure only D) verify reading and protect finished water quality
3. A filter has repeated short runs. Likely review should include: A) coagulation, flocculation, loading, backwash, and media condition B) office lighting C) operator seniority D) customer bills
4. A jar test shows best turbidity at a higher coagulant dose but lower pH. Next concern is: A) alkalinity/pH control and downstream impacts B) sample label color C) pump paint D) vehicle fuel
5. A filter turbidity spike occurs immediately after backwash. A useful control is: A) filter-to-waste or ripening procedure B) bypassing all monitoring C) raising pH blindly D) no action
6. CT is marginal during peak flow. Operator options include: A) ignore peak flow B) lower residual C) remove contact basin D) reduce flow, increase residual if allowed, increase storage/contact, or adjust operations
7. Clearwell level drops unexpectedly. First priority is: A) shut off alarms B) wait until morning C) paperwork only D) confirm cause and assess CT/storage/supply impacts
8. Chlorine residual leaving the plant suddenly drops. The operator should check: A) hydrant paint B) only office email C) analyzer, feed system, chemical strength, demand, flow, and sampling D) meter reading
9. A hypochlorite feed pump loses prime. The immediate concern is: A) loss of disinfectant feed B) high hardness only C) high nitrate D) DBP overcompliance
10. A chemical feed line ruptures. First priority is: A) finish paperwork first B) wash to storm drain C) safety, containment, isolation, notification, and treatment continuity D) ignore if small
11. A customer reports oily water. Best response is: A) tell them it is normal always B) no record C) treat as a potential contamination complaint and investigate promptly D) only flush next year
12. A single taste complaint may be less significant than: A) a normal pH B) a clustered pattern across an area or pressure zone C) an old map D) a routine log
13. A main break in a low-pressure area requires attention to: A) no bacteriological concern B) only asphalt repair C) intrusion risk, isolation, repair disinfection, flushing, and sampling D) changing operator grade
14. After a power failure, operators should verify: A) treatment continuity, alarms, pumps, residuals, pressure, and backup systems B) nothing if power returns C) only building lights D) only clock time
15. SCADA communication fails but plant continues running. Best action is: A) switch to local/manual monitoring procedures and restore communications B) stop all samples C) leave site D) assume all values are perfect
16. A chlorine analyzer reads zero but grab sample shows 1.2 mg/L. Likely next step: A) troubleshoot/calibrate analyzer while relying on verified grab checks B) increase chlorine wildly C) ignore both D) report no chlorine immediately without verification
17. If online pH differs greatly from bench pH, the operator should: A) verify calibration, sample location, and analyzer condition B) ignore bench data C) disable pH control D) average them blindly
18. A plant receives a chemical delivery. Before unloading, verify: A) chemical identity, delivery paperwork, tank, connections, and safety controls B) tank color only C) weather only D) driver music
19. Offloading the wrong chemical can cause: A) improved treatment always B) severe safety and treatment consequences C) no issue if diluted D) lower risk
20. A coagulant overdose may cause: A) no impacts B) perfect water always C) poor water quality, pH depression, residual metals, or filter issues D) zero sludge
21. A coagulant underdose may cause: A) poor floc and higher turbidity B) automatic disinfection C) lower turbidity always D) no filter impact
22. A filter backwash valve fails closed. The likely concern is: A) high fluoride B) inability to clean filter properly C) extra CT D) lower water age
23. A filter backwash waste valve fails open. The likely concern is: A) better production B) no concern C) water loss and possible process/control issue D) higher storage
24. A raw water pump vibration alarm should prompt: A) lower chlorine B) more coagulant C) ignore until failure D) inspection, trend review, and possible shutdown before damage
25. Bearing overheating may indicate: A) lubrication, alignment, load, or mechanical problems B) low nitrate C) good pump condition always D) high CT
26. A sudden pressure drop in distribution may indicate: A) main break, pump/tank issue, valve change, or demand surge B) stable operation always C) better residual D) lower risk
27. A closed valve after maintenance should be documented because: A) documents are decorative B) no one needs to know C) valve position affects hydraulics and response D) it prevents all breaks
28. Unauthorized access at a tank site should trigger: A) lowering chlorine B) security response and sanitary assessment C) only repainting D) no action
29. A bird or animal found inside a finished-water tank requires: A) immediate sanitary response, isolation/evaluation, disinfection/sampling, and notification as needed B) billing entry C) routine note only D) no concern if small
30. A positive E. coli result should not be treated as: A) routine aesthetic complaint B) investigation trigger C) regulatory matter D) potential acute health concern
31. A monitoring violation means: A) required sample/reporting was missed or invalid B) pump failed C) bill is late D) water is always unsafe
32. A treatment technique violation means: A) customer called B) operator forgot lunch C) required treatment/process control was not met D) sample bottle broke only
33. An MCL violation means: A) lower turbidity B) only aesthetics C) missing SDS D) contaminant limit was exceeded according to compliance rules
34. An action level exceedance for lead/copper is different from an MCL because it: A) triggers required actions rather than functioning as a standard MCL B) is only a lab typo C) requires no response D) applies to raw water only
35. A Tier 1 notice requires rapid communication because: A) it is only aesthetic B) immediate health risk may exist C) it is optional D) it is annual only
36. Operational data should be reviewed before making major dose changes because: A) only lab data matters yearly B) dose changes never matter C) trends are useless D) trends help prevent overcorrection
37. Multiple small abnormal signs together should be treated as: A) less important than one sign B) random only C) reason to stop logs D) possible developing failure pattern
38. A good troubleshooting sequence is: A) blame, ignore, repeat B) guess, adjust, leave C) verify, isolate, diagnose, correct, document, monitor D) alarm, silence, forget
39. If two instruments disagree, the operator should: A) choose the higher number always B) verify with independent check and calibration status C) choose the lower number always D) ignore both
40. If a process change solves one issue but creates another, the operator should: A) stop sampling B) hide results C) balance compliance goals and reassess the whole treatment train D) keep going blindly
41. A change in source water may require: A) automatic compliance B) treatment adjustment and more monitoring C) no action ever D) lower records
42. A high TOC source may increase risk of: A) no taste issue B) DBP formation C) lower coagulant demand always D) lower chlorine demand always
43. Enhanced coagulation is intended to improve: A) organic precursor removal for DBP control B) pump bearing life C) tank level control D) hydrant pressure only
44. If DBPs are high in far reaches of the system, consider: A) only repainting tanks B) water age, residual strategy, precursor removal, storage operation C) no treatment link D) billing routes
45. Lowering chlorine to reduce DBPs can create risk if it: A) increases CT always B) compromises microbial protection/residual C) removes all bacteria D) lowers all risk
46. Switching disinfectants can affect: A) sample labels only B) corrosion, nitrification, DBPs, residual stability, and monitoring needs C) only color of chemical tank D) operator grade
47. PFAS monitoring detections should lead to: A) no notes B) source evaluation, communication, and treatment/regulatory planning C) immediate denial D) only chlorine increase
48. Microplastics are best treated as: A) an emerging, measurable concern with evolving standards and methods B) impossible to test in any way C) irrelevant forever D) solved only by chlorine
49. A T5-level operator is expected to think in terms of: A) single valve only B) no regulations C) no documentation D) system-wide risk, compliance, staff/process control, and public health
50. The final decision in a plant upset should prioritize: A) reducing paperwork B) safe water and regulatory compliance over production convenience C) production at all costs D) hiding alarms
