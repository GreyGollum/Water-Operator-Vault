# T5 PFAS and Emerging Contaminants Mini Exam

> Original T5-style study questions for [[T5 Study Hub]]. This mini-exam focuses on PFAS, PFOA, PFOS, related compounds, treatment choices, monitoring concepts, public communication, and emerging contaminants such as microplastics and cyanotoxins.

## Current-Regulatory Caution

PFAS rules are moving. As of the latest check for this note:

- EPA's 2024 PFAS National Primary Drinking Water Regulation set enforceable MCLs for PFOA and PFOS at **4.0 ppt / ng/L**, with additional MCLs for PFHxS, PFNA, HFPO-DA/GenX, and a Hazard Index for mixtures.
- In 2026, EPA had proposed actions that would keep the PFOA/PFOS MCLs while potentially extending compliance timing, and separately proposed rescinding/reconsidering several other PFAS provisions.
- California DDW revised PFAS notification and response levels on **October 29, 2025**, including PFOA, PFOS, PFHxS, and PFHxA.
- Microplastics are a real measurable research and monitoring issue, but there is not a single simple routine compliance method or federal drinking-water MCL in the same way there is for regulated chemicals.

9. 4 ppt is equivalent to: A) 4 ng/L B) 4 mg/L C) 4 gpm D) 4 NTU
10. 1 µg/L equals: A) 1 ng/L B) 10 ng/L C) 100 ng/L D) 1,000 ng/L

21. PFAS treatment commonly includes: A) granular activated carbon, ion exchange, and reverse osmosis/nanofiltration B) sedimentation alone C) standard chlorination alone D) fluoridation alone

24. Reverse osmosis removes PFAS by: A) pressure-driven membrane separation B) floc blanket settling only C) chlorine demand D) corrosion coupons
25. A key operational concern for GAC PFAS treatment is: A) media breakthrough and replacement/regeneration planning B) fluoride color C) hydrant caps D) tank altitude only
26. A key operational concern for ion exchange PFAS treatment is: A) resin exhaustion, selectivity, regeneration/disposal issues B) jar-test paddles only C) chlorine gas pressure D) finished-water temperature only
27. A key operational concern for RO PFAS treatment is: A) concentrate disposal and membrane management B) filter-to-waste only C) backflow testing only D) pH meter color
28. Standard chlorine disinfection is generally: A) not considered a primary PFAS removal method B) the best PFAS destruction method in distribution C) required to convert PFAS to lead D) enough to remove all forever chemicals
29. Blending may reduce delivered concentration but: A) does not destroy or remove the contaminant mass B) destroys PFAS chemically C) eliminates monitoring need D) works without compliance review
30. Source substitution can be useful when: A) another approved source has lower contaminant levels and capacity is available B) no regulator coordination is needed C) all sources have identical chemistry D) the contaminated source is the only source
31. A T5-level PFAS decision should consider: A) public health, compliance, source capacity, treatment feasibility, residuals, waste streams, and communication B) only taste C) only pump color D) only daily production target
32. PFAS compounds differ from each other in: A) chain length, functional group, mobility, treatability, and toxicity data B) only spelling C) only color D) only pH
33. Short-chain PFAS can be harder for some adsorptive media because they may be: A) more mobile and less strongly adsorbed B) easier to settle by gravity C) destroyed by visible light D) converted to turbidity

35. PFNA is: A) a PFAS compound B) nitrate as nitrogen C) a pump bearing D) a disinfectant residual
36. HFPO-DA / GenX is: A) a replacement PFAS associated with fluoropolymer manufacturing B) a coliform test C) a filter media size D) a storage tank coating inspection only
37. PFBS is: A) a shorter-chain PFAS included in certain mixture/hazard-index discussions B) a chlorine residual C) a bacteriological sample type D) a pressure unit

## Quick Review - Compounds and Examples

| Item | What it is | Possible source / association | Treatment / control concept |
|---|---|---|---|
| PFOA | Long-chain PFAS carboxylic acid | Industrial use, legacy manufacturing, contaminated source water | GAC, IX, RO/NF; source control |
| PFOS | Long-chain PFAS sulfonic acid | AFFF firefighting foam, industrial legacy sources | GAC, IX, RO/NF; source control |
| PFHxS | PFAS sulfonic acid | AFFF/industrial sources | GAC, IX, RO/NF; source control |
| PFHxA | Shorter-chain PFAS carboxylic acid | Replacement chemistry/industrial sources | More challenging for some adsorptive media; verify design |
| PFNA | PFAS carboxylic acid | Industrial/fluorochemical sources | GAC, IX, RO/NF depending on design |
| HFPO-DA / GenX | Replacement PFAS | Fluoropolymer manufacturing | Advanced treatment and source control |
| PFBS | Short-chain PFAS sulfonic acid | Replacement chemistry | May be more mobile; treatment design matters |
| Microplastics | Small plastic particles | Plastic breakdown, textiles, tires, packaging, environmental debris | Particle removal processes may help; methods/regulation evolving |
| Cyanotoxins | Toxins from cyanobacteria/HABs | Reservoir blooms, warm nutrient-rich source waters | Source control, oxidation/adsorption/filtration strategy, monitoring |
| 1,4-Dioxane | Synthetic solvent/stabilizer contaminant | Industrial solvent use, landfill/leachate impacts | Advanced oxidation often considered |
| Perchlorate | Inorganic contaminant | Rocket fuel, fireworks, industrial sources, some natural occurrence | Ion exchange, biological treatment, source management |
