# T5 Flash Cards - PFAS and MCLs

> Format: Front = Question | Back = Answer

## Verification Notice

This deck is **partially verified**, not final-certified. Use the following source caveats during study:

- Federal MCL values are checked against [[Water Operator Source Bibliography#EPA-NPDWR]].
- Federal PFAS values are checked against [[Water Operator Source Bibliography#EPA-PFAS-NPDWR]], but PFAS values are date-sensitive.
- California PFAS notification/response-level values are tracked in [[PFAS Database]].
- Lead uses an **Action Level**, not a normal MCL. See [[Lead and Copper Rule Transition Note]].

## PFAS Fundamentals

### Card 1
**Front:** What does PFAS stand for?
**Back:** Per- and Polyfluoroalkyl Substances.

### Card 2
**Front:** Why are PFAS called "Forever Chemicals"?
**Back:** They are highly persistent and resist natural breakdown.

### Card 3
**Front:** What does PFOA stand for?
**Back:** Perfluorooctanoic Acid.

### Card 4
**Front:** What does PFOS stand for?
**Back:** Perfluorooctane Sulfonic Acid.

### Card 5
**Front:** What are common PFAS sources?
**Back:** Firefighting foam (AFFF), industrial manufacturing, stain-resistant products, nonstick coatings, grease-resistant packaging.

### Card 6
**Front:** PFAS concentrations are commonly reported in what unit?
**Back:** ppt (parts per trillion) or ng/L.

### Card 7
**Front:** 1 ppt equals what concentration in water?
**Back:** Approximately 1 ng/L.

### Card 8
**Front:** What laboratory methods are commonly used for PFAS?
**Back:** EPA Method 533 and EPA Method 537.1 using LC-MS/MS.

### Card 9
**Front:** Name three common PFAS treatment technologies.
**Back:** Granular Activated Carbon (GAC), Ion Exchange (IX), Reverse Osmosis/Nanofiltration.

### Card 10
**Front:** Does chlorination effectively remove PFAS?
**Back:** No. Standard chlorination is not considered a PFAS removal technology.

## Current PFAS Regulatory Cards

### Card 11
**Front:** Current EPA MCL for PFOA?
**Back:** 4 ppt / 4 ng/L under EPA's 2024 final PFAS NPDWR. Recheck before exam day because EPA announced proposed PFAS rule changes in 2026. Source: [[Water Operator Source Bibliography#EPA-PFAS-NPDWR]].

### Card 12
**Front:** Current EPA MCL for PFOS?
**Back:** 4 ppt / 4 ng/L under EPA's 2024 final PFAS NPDWR. Recheck before exam day because EPA announced proposed PFAS rule changes in 2026. Source: [[Water Operator Source Bibliography#EPA-PFAS-NPDWR]].

### Card 13
**Front:** What PFAS are included in EPA's Hazard Index approach?
**Back:** Under the 2024 final PFAS NPDWR: PFHxS, PFNA, HFPO-DA (GenX), and PFBS. This is date-sensitive because EPA announced proposed reconsideration/rescission of some non-PFOA/PFOS provisions in 2026.

### Card 14
**Front:** What does MCL stand for?
**Back:** Maximum Contaminant Level.

### Card 15
**Front:** What does MCLG stand for?
**Back:** Maximum Contaminant Level Goal.

### Card 16
**Front:** Is an MCL enforceable?
**Back:** Yes.

### Card 17
**Front:** Is an MCLG enforceable?
**Back:** No. It is a health-based goal.

### Card 18
**Front:** What federal law authorizes drinking water MCLs?
**Back:** Safe Drinking Water Act (SDWA).

### Card 19
**Front:** Who establishes national drinking water standards?
**Back:** EPA.

### Card 20
**Front:** Who enforces California drinking water regulations?
**Back:** California State Water Resources Control Board Division of Drinking Water.

## Treatment Cards

### Card 36
**Front:** What does GAC stand for?
**Back:** Granular Activated Carbon.

### Card 37
**Front:** How does GAC remove PFAS?
**Back:** Adsorption.

### Card 38
**Front:** Primary concern with GAC systems?
**Back:** Media breakthrough.

### Card 39
**Front:** Primary concern with ion exchange systems?
**Back:** Resin exhaustion.

### Card 40
**Front:** Primary concern with RO systems?
**Back:** Concentrate disposal and membrane maintenance.

### Card 41
**Front:** What is breakthrough?
**Back:** When contaminant begins passing through exhausted treatment media.

### Card 42
**Front:** What is blending?
**Back:** Mixing water sources to lower contaminant concentration.

### Card 43
**Front:** Does blending remove contaminant mass?
**Back:** No.

### Card 44
**Front:** What treatment technology generally provides the highest PFAS removal?
**Back:** Reverse Osmosis/Nanofiltration.

### Card 45
**Front:** Which PFAS are often more difficult for adsorptive media to remove?
**Back:** Short-chain PFAS.

## Emerging Contaminant Cards

### Card 46
**Front:** What are microplastics?
**Back:** Small plastic particles found in the environment and water.

### Card 47
**Front:** Are microplastics real and measurable?
**Back:** Yes.

### Card 48
**Front:** Do microplastics currently have a federal drinking water MCL?
**Back:** No. This remains an emerging contaminant issue rather than a standard federal MCL compliance item.

### Card 49
**Front:** Why are microplastics difficult to regulate?
**Back:** Sampling methods, particle sizes, exposure interpretation, and health data are still evolving.

### Card 50
**Front:** Name a treatment process that may reduce microplastics.
**Back:** Coagulation, sedimentation, filtration, or membrane treatment.

# Section 1 - Safe Drinking Water Act

### Card 1
Front: What does SDWA stand for?
Back: Safe Drinking Water Act.

### Card 2
Front: What is the primary federal law governing public drinking water systems?
Back: Safe Drinking Water Act.

### Card 3
Front: What federal agency administers the SDWA?
Back: EPA.

### Card 4
Front: What is the primary purpose of the SDWA?
Back: Protect public health through safe drinking water.

### Card 5
Front: What is an MCL?
Back: Maximum Contaminant Level; the enforceable highest contaminant level allowed in drinking water. Source: [[Water Operator Source Bibliography#EPA-NPDWR]].

### Card 6
Front: What is an MCLG?
Back: Maximum Contaminant Level Goal; a non-enforceable health goal. Source: [[Water Operator Source Bibliography#EPA-NPDWR]].

### Card 7
Front: Which is enforceable: MCL or MCLG?
Back: MCL.

### Card 8
Front: What is a treatment technique?
Back: A required treatment process intended to reduce contaminant levels when a numeric MCL is not the main compliance tool. Source: [[Water Operator Source Bibliography#EPA-NPDWR]].

### Card 9
Front: Give an example of a treatment technique.
Back: Surface Water Treatment Rule filtration/turbidity requirements.

### Card 10
Front: Why are treatment techniques used?
Back: Some risks are controlled best through process performance rather than contaminant testing alone.

### Card 11
Front: What is primacy?
Back: EPA-approved state authority to implement drinking-water regulations at least as stringent as federal requirements.

### Card 12
Front: Does California have drinking-water primacy?
Back: Yes; California implements drinking-water regulation through the State Water Resources Control Board Division of Drinking Water.

### Card 13
Front: Which California agency oversees drinking water regulation?
Back: State Water Resources Control Board Division of Drinking Water.

### Card 14
Front: What is a Public Water System?
Back: A system serving at least 15 service connections or 25 people for at least 60 days per year. Verify exact legal wording during eCFR/Title 22 audit.

### Card 15
Front: Community Water System definition?
Back: Serves the same population year-round.

### Card 16
Front: Non-Transient Non-Community System definition?
Back: Serves the same people regularly but not as residents.

### Card 17
Front: Transient Non-Community System definition?
Back: Serves changing populations such as parks or campgrounds.

### Card 18
Front: What is a sanitary survey?
Back: Comprehensive review of facilities, operations, source, treatment, distribution, monitoring, and management.

### Card 19
Front: Why are sanitary surveys important?
Back: They identify deficiencies before they become health risks.

### Card 20
Front: What is the ultimate goal of drinking water regulations?
Back: Protection of public health.

# Section 2 - Public Notification

### Card 21
Front: What is Public Notification?
Back: Required communication to customers about violations or situations that may pose drinking-water risk.

### Card 22
Front: How many Public Notification tiers exist?
Back: Three. Source: [[Water Operator Source Bibliography#EPA-PUBLIC-NOTIFICATION]].

### Card 23
Front: What is a Tier 1 Public Notice?
Back: Immediate/acute health-risk notice; EPA lists Tier 1 timing as 24 hours. Source: [[Water Operator Source Bibliography#EPA-PUBLIC-NOTIFICATION]].

### Card 24
Front: Example of Tier 1 event?
Back: Acute microbial concern such as E. coli contamination or loss of treatment/disinfection. Verify exact trigger wording during eCFR audit.

### Card 25
Front: What is a Tier 2 Public Notice?
Back: Non-immediate but health-significant notice; EPA lists timing as as soon as possible, but within 30 days. Source: [[Water Operator Source Bibliography#EPA-PUBLIC-NOTIFICATION]].

### Card 26
Front: What is a Tier 3 Public Notice?
Back: Lower-risk notice such as monitoring/reporting issues; EPA lists timing as up to one year. Source: [[Water Operator Source Bibliography#EPA-PUBLIC-NOTIFICATION]].

### Card 27
Front: What is a Boil Water Notice?
Back: Notice instructing consumers to boil water before use due to possible microbial risk.

### Card 28
Front: What is the purpose of Public Notification?
Back: Inform customers so they can protect themselves.

### Card 29
Front: What regulation governs annual water quality reporting?
Back: Consumer Confidence Report Rule.

### Card 30
Front: What is a CCR?
Back: Consumer Confidence Report.

### Card 31
Front: How often is a CCR issued?
Back: Annually for Community Water Systems. Verify exact delivery timing during CCR audit.

### Card 32
Front: What information is included in a CCR?
Back: Water quality results, source information, violations, and health information.

### Card 33
Front: Who receives CCRs?
Back: Customers of Community Water Systems.

### Card 34
Front: Why are CCRs important?
Back: Transparency and public awareness.

### Card 35
Front: What should operators understand about CCR data?
Back: It reflects compliance history and customer communication obligations.

# Section 3 - Monitoring and Compliance

### Card 36
Front: What is a monitoring violation?
Back: Failure to collect, submit, or properly analyze required samples.

### Card 37
Front: Does a monitoring violation automatically mean unsafe water?
Back: No, but it means required water quality confirmation was not completed.

### Card 38
Front: Why are monitoring violations still serious?
Back: Water quality cannot be verified as required.

### Card 39
Front: What is a reporting violation?
Back: Required information was not submitted correctly or on time.

### Card 40
Front: What is an MCL violation?
Back: Exceeding an enforceable regulatory contaminant limit.

### Card 41
Front: What is a treatment technique violation?
Back: Failure to meet required treatment/process performance.

### Card 42
Front: Which is generally more serious: MCL or reporting violation?
Back: MCL violation, because it may reflect direct contaminant exceedance; exact PN tier depends on contaminant and rule.

### Card 43
Front: What is compliance monitoring?
Back: Sampling required to demonstrate regulatory compliance.

### Card 44
Front: Why is documentation critical?
Back: If it is not documented, it is hard to prove it happened.

### Card 45
Front: What should records be?
Back: Accurate, complete, timely, and traceable.

# Section 4 - Surface Water Rules

### Card 46
Front: What is the purpose of the Surface Water Treatment Rule?
Back: Control microbial pathogens in surface water and GWUDI systems.

### Card 47
Front: What pathogens are major SWTR targets?
Back: Giardia, viruses, and Cryptosporidium.

### Card 48
Front: Why is turbidity important?
Back: It indicates treatment effectiveness and particle/pathogen-removal performance.

### Card 49
Front: What is CT?
Back: Disinfectant concentration × effective contact time.

### Card 50
Front: Why is CT important?
Back: Demonstrates pathogen inactivation.

### Card 51
Front: What does T10 represent?
Back: Effective contact time used for CT calculations.

### Card 52
Front: Why is baffling important?
Back: Improves effective contact time and reduces short-circuiting.

### Card 53
Front: What is filter breakthrough?
Back: Particles passing through a filter.

### Card 54
Front: What is filter-to-waste?
Back: Diverting initial filter effluent after backwash until water quality stabilizes.

### Card 55
Front: Why monitor turbidity continuously?
Back: Early warning of treatment problems and barrier performance.

# Section 6 - Lead and Copper Rule

### Card 66
Front: What is the purpose of the Lead and Copper Rule?
Back: Control lead and copper exposure from corrosion of plumbing materials.

### Card 67
Front: Lead Action Level?
Back: Lead is an Action Level item, not a standard MCL. Older/current LCR summary material commonly lists 15 ppb / 15 µg/L, while EPA's newer NPDWR/LCRI context lists 0.010 mg/L / 10 µg/L. Verify current California exam expectation and implementation date before exam day. See [[Lead and Copper Rule Transition Note]].

### Card 68
Front: Copper Action Level?
Back: 1.3 mg/L. Source: [[Water Operator Source Bibliography#EPA-NPDWR]] and [[Water Operator Source Bibliography#EPA-LCR]].

### Card 69
Front: Are lead and copper regulated by MCLs?
Back: No. They are controlled through Action Levels and treatment/corrosion-control requirements.

### Card 70
Front: What happens when Action Levels are exceeded?
Back: Required corrective actions are triggered; exact action depends on rule version and implementation timing.

### Card 71
Front: Where are lead and copper samples typically collected?
Back: Customer taps.

### Card 72
Front: Why first-draw samples?
Back: They capture plumbing exposure after stagnation.

### Card 73
Front: Primary lead source?
Back: Corrosion of plumbing materials.

### Card 74
Front: Primary copper source?
Back: Corrosion of copper plumbing.

### Card 75
Front: Common corrosion-control method?
Back: Orthophosphate and pH/alkalinity control, depending on system-specific corrosion study and regulatory requirements.

### Card 82

### Card 84

### Card 85

Back: Write the formula first, then substitute values.

Back: Parts per hundred.

### Card 66

### Card 70

Back: 2.0 mg/L.

### Card 71
Front: If required CT is 90 and residual is 3.0 mg/L, required time is?
Back: 30 minutes.

### Card 72
Front: What is T10?
Back: Effective contact time often based on tracer behavior; actual method must follow the applicable guidance/table approach.

### Card 73
Front: Why use effective time instead of theoretical time?
Back: It better accounts for short-circuiting.

### Card 74
Front: What is theoretical detention time?
Back: Volume divided by flow.

### Card 75
Front: What is baffling factor?
Back: Factor used to estimate effective contact time from theoretical detention time.

### Card 76
Front: Effective contact time formula using baffling factor?
Back: T10 = theoretical detention time × baffling factor.

### Card 77
Front: A 100-minute theoretical time and 0.5 baffling factor gives T10?
Back: 50 minutes.

### Card 78
Front: Higher flow through the same basin does what to detention time?
Back: Decreases detention time.

### Card 79
Front: Lower clearwell level usually does what to contact time?
Back: Reduces available contact volume and time.

### Card 80
Front: What information is needed to evaluate CT?
Back: Residual, effective contact time, pH, temperature, disinfectant type, and organism target.

### Card 81
Front: Why do pH and temperature matter for CT tables?
Back: They affect disinfectant effectiveness.

### Card 82
Front: What happens to chlorine CT requirement in colder water?
Back: Required CT generally increases.

### Card 83
Front: What happens to free chlorine CT requirement at higher pH?
Back: Required CT generally increases.

### Card 84
Front: Actual CT must be what compared to required CT?
Back: Equal to or greater than required CT.

### Card 85
Front: Actual CT 80, required CT 70. Status?
Back: Passing.

### Card 86
Front: Actual CT 55, required CT 70. Status?
Back: Failing.

### Card 87
Front: Best first response to apparent CT failure?
Back: Verify data, protect finished water, adjust operations, and notify/escalate as required.

### Card 88
Front: What operational changes can improve CT?
Back: Increase effective time, increase residual if allowed, reduce flow, improve baffling, or increase contact volume.

### Card 89
Front: What can reduce CT margin during peak demand?
Back: Higher flow and lower storage/contact time.

### Card 90
Front: CT is close to failing during high flow. What should be reviewed?
Back: Flow, clearwell level, T10, residual, pH, temperature, and operating constraints.

# Section - Coagulation

### Card 1
Front: What is coagulation?
Back: The chemical destabilization of suspended and colloidal particles so they can clump together.

### Card 2
Front: What is the main purpose of coagulation?
Back: To make small particles easier to remove by flocculation, sedimentation, and filtration.

### Card 3
Front: What is a colloid?
Back: A very small suspended particle that does not settle easily by gravity.

### Card 4
Front: Why do colloids resist settling?
Back: They often have surface charges that repel each other and keep them dispersed.

### Card 5
Front: What does charge neutralization do?
Back: Reduces particle repulsion so particles can come together.

### Card 6
Front: What is sweep floc?
Back: A mechanism where metal hydroxide precipitate forms and enmeshes particles.

### Card 7
Front: Common aluminum-based coagulant?
Back: Alum, also called aluminum sulfate.

### Card 8
Front: Common iron-based coagulants?
Back: Ferric chloride and ferric sulfate.

### Card 9
Front: What is PACl?
Back: Polyaluminum chloride, a pre-hydrolyzed aluminum coagulant.

### Card 10
Front: What is a coagulant aid?
Back: A chemical, often polymer, used to improve floc formation or settling.

### Card 11
Front: Why does pH matter in coagulation?
Back: Coagulants work best within certain pH ranges and can change water chemistry.

### Card 12
Front: How can alum affect alkalinity?
Back: Alum consumes alkalinity and can lower pH.

### Card 13
Front: What happens if alkalinity is too low during coagulation?
Back: pH can drop and coagulation performance can suffer.

### Card 14
Front: What chemical can be used to add alkalinity?
Back: Lime, caustic soda, soda ash, or sodium bicarbonate depending on system design.

### Card 15
Front: What is enhanced coagulation?
Back: Optimizing coagulation to remove more natural organic matter and DBP precursors.

### Card 16
Front: What is TOC?
Back: Total Organic Carbon, a measure of organic material in water.

### Card 17
Front: Why remove TOC before disinfection?
Back: TOC can react with disinfectants to form DBPs.

### Card 18
Front: What is a jar test?
Back: A bench-scale test used to evaluate coagulant dose, pH, mixing, floc formation, and settling.

### Card 19
Front: What should be observed during a jar test?
Back: Floc formation, settling rate, final turbidity, pH, and treatment goals.

### Card 20
Front: What does poor pin floc indicate?
Back: Possible underdosing, wrong pH, poor mixing, or source water change.

### Card 21
Front: What can coagulant overdose cause?
Back: Poor turbidity removal, pH depression, residual metals, filter problems, and excess sludge.

### Card 22
Front: What can coagulant underdose cause?
Back: Poor particle destabilization and high settled or filtered turbidity.

### Card 23
Front: What is rapid mix?
Back: High-energy mixing to disperse coagulant quickly through raw water.

### Card 24
Front: Why must rapid mix be fast?
Back: Coagulant reactions occur quickly and need immediate dispersion.

### Card 25
Front: A sudden raw water turbidity increase should trigger what?
Back: Jar testing and process evaluation.

### Card 26
Front: What source change may increase coagulant demand?

# T5 Flash Cards - Lab Procedures and Methods

> Operator-level flashcards for drinking-water and water/wastewater lab methods. These cards are for study and method recognition, not certified-lab SOP replacement.

## Compliance Authority

**Front:** For drinking-water compliance monitoring, what source takes precedence over EPA method-summary tables?  
**Back:** The current CFR and the incorporated-by-reference approved method text. EPA tables are useful summaries, but legal compliance is controlled by the CFR, approved method version, and state primacy agency requirements.

**Front:** Are manufacturer inserts and product catalogs the highest authority for compliance monitoring?  
**Back:** No. They are useful for method/product understanding, but compliance use must match CFR-approved methods, exact method versions, state requirements, and certified-lab SOPs.

**Front:** Why should an operator avoid saying “this kit is approved” without context?  
**Back:** A kit or method may be approved only for certain analytes, matrices, rules, states, method versions, or reporting contexts. Always verify the approved method and use case.

## RTCR / Microbiology

**Front:** Under RTCR, is routine total coliform/E. coli monitoring usually presence/absence or density/count based?  
**Back:** Presence/absence is sufficient for routine RTCR total coliform/E. coli compliance. Density is not required for routine RTCR samples.

**Front:** What is the standard RTCR microbiological sample volume?  
**Back:** 100 mL.

**Front:** What is the maximum collection-to-incubation window commonly emphasized for RTCR samples?  
**Back:** 30 hours maximum from sample collection to incubation.

**Front:** Why is sodium thiosulfate used in bottles for chlorinated microbiological samples?  
**Back:** It neutralizes chlorine residual so the disinfectant does not continue killing organisms before analysis.

**Front:** What is the difference between CFU and MPN?  
**Back:** CFU means colony-forming units, usually from plate-count methods. MPN means most probable number, a statistical estimate from positive tubes/wells/trays.

**Front:** What is HPC used for at an operator-study level?  
**Back:** HPC is a general indicator of heterotrophic bacterial growth and can help track biological activity or water-quality changes. It is not the same as total coliform/E. coli compliance testing.

## QA/QC and Lab Control

**Front:** What is precision?  
**Back:** Precision is reproducibility: how close repeated results are to each other under controlled conditions.

**Front:** What is accuracy?  
**Back:** Accuracy is closeness to the true or accepted value, often checked by recovery of a known addition or reference standard.

**Front:** Can data be precise but inaccurate?  
**Back:** Yes. Repeated results can agree with each other but all be biased away from the true value due to systematic error.

**Front:** What is a blank used for?  
**Back:** A blank checks contamination or background contribution from reagents, water, containers, or the method process.

**Front:** What is a duplicate or replicate used for?  
**Back:** It checks repeatability/precision of sampling or analysis.

**Front:** What is a spike or known addition used for?  
**Back:** It helps evaluate accuracy/recovery and whether the sample matrix affects the method.

**Front:** Why should calibration and checks be documented?  
**Back:** Documentation proves the instrument or method was under control when results were produced.

## Reagents, Standards, and Lab Water

**Front:** What is an analytical standard?  
**Back:** A known-quality material used to calibrate or check a method so results reflect the sample composition reliably.

**Front:** Why should expired reagents generally not be used for compliance or critical testing?  
**Back:** Reagent strength or purity may change over time, causing biased or invalid results.

**Front:** Why is reagent water quality important?  
**Back:** Contaminants in reagent water can add background, interfere with reactions, or bias low-level analyses.

## Glassware, Volumetric Devices, and Balances

**Front:** Why should volumetric glassware be used correctly instead of estimating with beaker marks?  
**Back:** Beaker marks are approximate. Volumetric flasks, pipets, burets, and graduated cylinders have defined accuracy/tolerance.

**Front:** Why is the meniscus important in volume measurement?  
**Back:** Reading the meniscus correctly helps avoid systematic volume error.

**Front:** What is a buret commonly used for?  
**Back:** Delivering measured titrant volume during titration.

**Front:** Why should an analytical balance be level and protected from drafts/vibration?  
**Back:** Drafts, vibration, and leveling errors can affect mass readings.

## Wet Chemistry / Field Methods

**Front:** What is a titration?  
**Back:** A method where a reagent is added until an endpoint is reached; the volume used is converted to a concentration/result.

**Front:** What is an endpoint in titration?  
**Back:** The method-defined point where the reaction is considered complete, often indicated by color change or instrument response.

**Front:** What can cause titration error?  
**Back:** Wrong sample volume, wrong titrant strength, misread endpoint, air bubbles, expired reagent, poor mixing, or wrong units.

**Front:** What does a colorimetric test measure?  
**Back:** It measures color intensity from a chemical reaction, visually or by instrument, to estimate concentration.

**Front:** What is a turbidimetric method based on?  
**Back:** Measuring cloudiness/light scatter caused by suspended or precipitated particles.

**Front:** What do pH and ion-selective electrodes require before reliable use?  
**Back:** Proper calibration, clean/properly stored probes, stable readings, and appropriate standards.

## Common Method Families

**Front:** Which common lab method family is used for hardness in many training references?  
**Back:** EDTA titration.

**Front:** Which method family is commonly associated with alkalinity?  
**Back:** Titration, often potentiometric or color-endpoint depending on method.

**Front:** Which method family is commonly associated with residual chlorine in operator testing?  
**Back:** DPD colorimetric methods and amperometric titration are common method families; compliance use depends on approved method/context.

**Front:** Which field/lab parameter is commonly measured by nephelometric methods?  
**Back:** Turbidity.

## Safety and SOP Caveats

**Front:** Do these lab flashcards replace a site SOP or certified lab method?  
**Back:** No. They are operator study aids only. Always follow approved methods, site SOPs, SDS, permits, and regulatory requirements.

**Front:** Why should an operator know lab-method concepts even if a certified lab performs the test?  
**Back:** Operators must understand sampling, preservation, result meaning, invalidation risks, reporting urgency, and operational response.

# T5 Flash Cards - Cross Connection Control

> Operator-level flashcards for cross-connection control. Use local rules and approved tester manuals for actual installation and field testing.

## Basics

**Front:** What is a cross-connection?  
**Back:** A physical or potential connection between potable water and a source of unknown or lesser quality.

**Front:** What is backflow?  
**Back:** Unwanted reverse flow into the potable water system.

**Front:** What is backsiphonage?  
**Back:** Reverse flow caused by reduced supply pressure.

**Front:** What is backpressure?  
**Back:** Reverse flow caused by downstream pressure greater than supply pressure.

**Front:** What controls protection selection?  
**Back:** Hazard level, backflow condition, local program rules, approved lists, and installation constraints.

**Front:** What is containment protection?  
**Back:** Protection at the service connection to protect the public water system.

**Front:** What is isolation protection?  
**Back:** Protection at or near the hazard inside the facility.

## Devices and Assemblies

**Front:** What is the strongest physical protection method?  
**Back:** A properly installed air gap.

**Front:** What does RP stand for?  
**Back:** Reduced-pressure principle assembly.

**Front:** What does DC stand for?  
**Back:** Double check valve assembly.

**Front:** What does PVB protect against?  
**Back:** Backsiphonage only, not backpressure.

**Front:** What does AVB protect against?  
**Back:** Backsiphonage only, usually point-of-use and not continuous pressure.

**Front:** Is a dual check the same as a testable DC assembly?  
**Back:** No. A dual check is not equivalent to a testable double check valve assembly.

## Double Check Assembly

**Front:** What are the main parts of a DC assembly under AWWA C510?  
**Back:** Two independently operating check valves, two shutoff valves, and four properly placed test cocks.

**Front:** What standard covers double check valve backflow prevention assemblies?  
**Back:** ANSI/AWWA C510.

**Front:** Does AWWA C510 mean AWWA certifies or approves every product?  
**Back:** No. AWWA standards describe minimum requirements and do not constitute product endorsement, testing, certification, or approval.

**Front:** Why are test cocks required on testable assemblies?  
**Back:** They allow field testing of the assembly.

**Front:** What does the flow arrow on an assembly show?  
**Back:** The required direction of flow through the assembly.

## Installation and Use

**Front:** Why must assemblies be accessible?  
**Back:** They must be testable, repairable, and maintainable.

**Front:** Why does RP drainage matter?  
**Back:** RP assemblies may discharge through the relief valve, so drainage and location must be planned.

**Front:** Why is an unprotected bypass a problem?  
**Back:** It can defeat the intended protection.

**Front:** Why is freeze/flood protection important?  
**Back:** Environmental damage can impair an assembly.

**Front:** Who controls actual testing and reporting requirements?  
**Back:** The local authority having jurisdiction and applicable state/program rules.

# T5 Flash Cards - Distribution Source Hydrants and Disinfection

> Source-supported operator-study flashcards from the 2026-06-19 distribution/source intake batch. These are study aids, not engineering specifications or local SOPs.

## Distribution Basics

**Front:** What is the main purpose of a drinking water distribution system?  
**Back:** To deliver potable water from source, treatment, or storage facilities to customers while maintaining adequate pressure, flow, and water quality.

**Front:** Name common components of a distribution system.  
**Back:** Mains, transmission lines, storage, pumps, valves, hydrants, meters, services, controls, and appurtenances.

**Front:** Why are looped distribution systems generally preferred over dead-end layouts?  
**Back:** Looped systems improve circulation, redundancy, pressure support, and operational flexibility.

**Front:** What water-quality issue is common in dead ends or low-flow areas?  
**Back:** Increased water age, disinfectant residual loss, sediment accumulation, and possible taste/odor or microbial concerns.

**Front:** What does AWWA G200 support in the vault?  
**Back:** Distribution-system operation and management program concepts, including water quality, O&M, documentation, employee training, and internal auditing.

**Front:** Do AWWA standards override laws, regulations, or local codes?  
**Back:** No. Applicable law, regulation, code, and authority having jurisdiction control.

## Pipes, Storage, Pumps, Valves

**Front:** What are transmission lines?  
**Back:** Larger pipelines used to move water between source, treatment, storage, and distribution areas.

**Front:** Why does pipe material selection matter?  
**Back:** Material affects pressure capacity, corrosion, installation, water quality, cost, service life, and compatibility with site conditions.

**Front:** What does pipe C value relate to?  
**Back:** Hydraulic roughness/friction characteristics used in flow and head-loss calculations.

**Front:** What is a storage facility used for in distribution?  
**Back:** Balancing demand, maintaining pressure, providing emergency/fire supply, and supporting operations.

**Front:** Why are valves important in a distribution system?  
**Back:** They allow isolation, control, flushing, maintenance, pressure management, and emergency response.

## Fire Hydrants

**Front:** What is the primary purpose of a fire hydrant?  
**Back:** Public fire protection service.

**Front:** What type of hydrant is intended for climates where freezing temperatures occur?  
**Back:** Dry-barrel fire hydrant.

**Front:** What AWWA standard covers dry-barrel fire hydrants?  
**Back:** ANSI/AWWA C502.

**Front:** Why should non-fire use of hydrants be restricted and controlled?  
**Back:** To keep hydrants in satisfactory working condition for fire emergencies and protect water quality.

**Front:** If a hydrant cannot be operated by one person using a proper hydrant wrench, what should happen?  
**Back:** It should be repaired rather than forced with excessive leverage.

**Front:** What AWWA manual is commonly paired with C502 for hydrant field maintenance?  
**Back:** AWWA Manual M17, Fire Hydrants: Installation, Field Testing, and Maintenance.

## Pipeline and Storage Disinfection

**Front:** Why is distribution-system disinfection important?  
**Back:** Distribution deficiencies can contribute to waterborne disease; disinfection and sanitary practices reduce contamination risk.

**Front:** What are common contamination routes during water main work?  
**Back:** Soil, sediment, foreign water, trench water, runoff, dirty appurtenances, exposed pipe interiors, and poor sanitary practices.

**Front:** Name the basic sequence for new pipeline disinfection work.  
**Back:** Inspection, sanitary construction, flushing/cleaning, backflow prevention during installation, chlorination, final flushing, bacteriological testing, connection, and documentation.

**Front:** What are common storage facility contamination routes?  
**Back:** Open/missing vents, hatches, doors, roof openings, inspection work, foreign matter, and sanitary defects.

**Front:** What AWWA standards are commonly tied to water main and storage facility disinfection?  
**Back:** AWWA C651 for water mains and AWWA C652 for water-storage facilities.

**Front:** Why is bacteriological testing part of pipeline/storage disinfection?  
**Back:** It helps verify sanitary conditions before the facility is returned to potable service.

## Chlorination / Chloramination

**Front:** What are common chemicals used for pipeline/storage disinfection?  
**Back:** Liquid chlorine, sodium hypochlorite, and calcium hypochlorite.

**Front:** Which free chlorine species is more effective as a disinfectant?  
**Back:** Hypochlorous acid, HOCl.

**Front:** What does higher pH do to free chlorine effectiveness?  
**Back:** Higher pH shifts free chlorine toward hypochlorite ion, which is less effective than hypochlorous acid.

**Front:** What does M20 support in this vault?  
**Back:** Chlorination/chloramination principles, chemical properties, feed systems, safety, strategies, and residual testing topics.

## Treatment Media and Chemicals

**Front:** What does AWWA B403 cover?  
**Back:** Aluminum sulfate, including liquid, ground, or lump forms.

**Front:** What is aluminum sulfate commonly used for in treatment?  
**Back:** As a coagulant to help destabilize particles and improve clarification/filtration.

**Front:** What does AWWA B600 cover?  
**Back:** Powdered activated carbon.

**Front:** What does AWWA B604 cover?  
**Back:** Granular activated carbon.

**Front:** What is activated carbon commonly used for?  
**Back:** Adsorption of taste-and-odor compounds and some organic contaminants.

**Front:** What does AWWA B112 cover?  
**Back:** Microfiltration and ultrafiltration membrane systems.

**Front:** What does AWWA B114 cover?  
**Back:** Reverse osmosis and nanofiltration systems for water treatment.

**Front:** Which membranes generally remove dissolved salts better: MF/UF or RO/NF?  
**Back:** RO/NF; MF/UF are primarily particle/microbial barrier processes, while RO/NF address dissolved material to a greater degree.

## Source Caveats

**Front:** Why is the Water Sources uploaded file caveated?  
**Back:** It includes valid AWWA front matter and table of contents, but also suspicious sales/testbank pages and unrelated parsed text, so integrity review is needed.

**Front:** Why should product standards be paired with operator/training references?  
**Back:** Product standards define minimum requirements for products/systems; they do not fully explain operations, troubleshooting, or site-specific design.

**Front:** What controls final installation, operation, and compliance decisions?  
**Back:** Current regulations, state/local requirements, authority having jurisdiction, engineering design, manufacturer instructions, and utility SOPs.
