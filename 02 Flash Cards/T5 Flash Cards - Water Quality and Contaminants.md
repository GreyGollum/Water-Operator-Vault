# Section 1 - Water Quality Fundamentals

### Card 1
Front: What is water quality?
Back: The physical, chemical, biological, and radiological characteristics of water.

### Card 2
Front: What are the four major contaminant categories?
Back: Physical, chemical, biological, and radiological.

### Card 3
Front: What is a primary drinking water standard?
Back: A health-based regulation.

### Card 4
Front: What is a secondary drinking water standard?
Back: An aesthetic standard affecting taste, odor, or appearance.

### Card 5
Front: Which is usually more important: primary or secondary standards?
Back: Primary standards because they protect health.

### Card 6
Front: What is turbidity?
Back: A measure of water cloudiness caused by suspended particles.

### Card 7
Front: Why is turbidity important?
Back: It can indicate reduced treatment effectiveness and pathogen risk.

### Card 8
Front: What unit is turbidity measured in?
Back: NTU.

### Card 9
Front: What is color in water usually caused by?
Back: Natural organic matter, metals, or industrial contamination.

### Card 10
Front: What causes taste and odor problems most often?
Back: Algae, organics, and biological activity.

# Section 2 - pH, Alkalinity, and Hardness

### Card 11
Front: What is pH?
Back: A measure of hydrogen ion concentration.

### Card 12
Front: pH 7 is considered what?
Back: Neutral.

### Card 13
Front: pH below 7 is?
Back: Acidic.

### Card 14
Front: pH above 7 is?
Back: Basic / alkaline.

### Card 15
Front: Why is pH important?
Back: It affects treatment, corrosion, and disinfection.

### Card 16
Front: What is alkalinity?
Back: Water's ability to resist pH changes.

### Card 17
Front: Why is alkalinity important?
Back: It helps stabilize pH.

### Card 18
Front: What is hardness?
Back: Calcium and magnesium concentration.

### Card 19
Front: What causes scale formation?
Back: Hard water and precipitation of minerals such as calcium carbonate.

### Card 20
Front: What can contribute to corrosive water?
Back: Low pH, low alkalinity, and aggressive water chemistry.

# Section 3 - Nitrate and Nitrite

### Card 21
Front: Nitrate MCL?
Back: 10 mg/L as nitrogen. Source: [[Water Operator Source Bibliography#EPA-NPDWR]].

### Card 22
Front: Nitrite MCL?
Back: 1 mg/L as nitrogen. Source: [[Water Operator Source Bibliography#EPA-NPDWR]].

### Card 23
Front: Common nitrate sources?
Back: Fertilizers, septic systems, animal waste, and agricultural runoff.

### Card 24
Front: Common nitrite source?
Back: Biological conversion processes, including nitrification/denitrification-related nitrogen cycling.

### Card 25
Front: Main health concern for nitrate?
Back: Methemoglobinemia / blue baby syndrome.

### Card 26
Front: Which population is most vulnerable to nitrate?
Back: Infants.

### Card 27
Front: What process converts ammonia to nitrate?
Back: Nitrification.

### Card 28
Front: What process converts nitrate to nitrogen gas?
Back: Denitrification.

### Card 29
Front: Common nitrate treatment?
Back: Ion exchange, biological treatment, reverse osmosis, or source management depending on system design.

### Card 30
Front: Why monitor nitrate carefully?
Back: It can create acute health risks.

# Section 4 - Arsenic

### Card 31
Front: Arsenic MCL?
Back: 10 µg/L. Source: [[Water Operator Source Bibliography#EPA-NPDWR]].

### Card 32
Front: Arsenic is classified as what type of contaminant?
Back: Inorganic chemical contaminant.

### Card 33
Front: Common arsenic source?
Back: Naturally occurring geologic formations and some industrial/agricultural sources.

### Card 34
Front: Long-term arsenic exposure risk?
Back: Increased cancer risk and other chronic health concerns.

### Card 35
Front: Common arsenic treatment?
Back: Adsorptive media, coagulation/filtration, ion exchange, reverse osmosis, or source management depending on arsenic species and water chemistry.

### Card 36
Front: Is arsenic usually visible?
Back: No.

### Card 37
Front: Why is arsenic challenging?
Back: Significant health risk at very low concentrations.

### Card 38
Front: Arsenic concentration is usually reported in?
Back: µg/L.

### Card 39
Front: Is arsenic a primary standard?
Back: Yes.

### Card 40
Front: Why is arsenic frequently tested?
Back: It is a regulated health contaminant.

# Section 5 - Lead and Copper

### Card 41
Front: Primary source of lead in drinking water?
Back: Corrosion of plumbing materials.

### Card 42
Front: Lead Action Level?
Back: Lead is an Action Level item, not a standard MCL. Older/current LCR summary material commonly lists 15 ppb / 15 µg/L, while EPA's newer NPDWR/LCRI context lists 0.010 mg/L / 10 µg/L. Verify current California exam expectation and implementation date before exam day. See [[Lead and Copper Rule Transition Note]].

### Card 43
Front: Copper Action Level?
Back: 1.3 mg/L. Source: [[Water Operator Source Bibliography#EPA-NPDWR]] and [[Water Operator Source Bibliography#EPA-LCR]].

### Card 44
Front: Are lead and copper usually source-water problems?
Back: No, they are often plumbing/corrosion-related.

### Card 45
Front: Main lead health concern?
Back: Neurological and developmental harm, especially for children.

### Card 46
Front: Children are particularly vulnerable to?
Back: Lead exposure.

### Card 47
Front: What treatment minimizes lead release?
Back: Corrosion control.

### Card 48
Front: Common corrosion inhibitor?
Back: Orthophosphate or other phosphate-based corrosion control, depending on system-specific treatment strategy.

### Card 49
Front: Why monitor lead at customer taps?
Back: Exposure often occurs from premise plumbing and service lines.

### Card 50
Front: What is the operator goal with lead?
Back: Minimize corrosion and exposure while meeting current regulatory requirements.

# Section 6 - Iron and Manganese

### Card 51
Front: Iron is regulated as what type of standard?
Back: Secondary standard / aesthetic contaminant in typical drinking-water study context.

### Card 52
Front: Iron commonly causes what issue?
Back: Red-brown staining.

### Card 53
Front: Manganese commonly causes what issue?
Back: Black staining.

### Card 54
Front: Iron source?
Back: Natural geology, corrosion, and source-water conditions.

### Card 55
Front: Manganese source?
Back: Natural geology and source-water conditions.

### Card 56
Front: Why remove iron and manganese?
Back: Customer complaints, staining, taste/odor, and operational issues.

### Card 57
Front: Common treatment method?
Back: Oxidation followed by filtration, depending on chemistry and plant design.

### Card 58
Front: Can manganese affect water color?
Back: Yes.

### Card 59
Front: Can iron foul equipment?
Back: Yes.

### Card 60
Front: Iron and manganese are primarily what concern?
Back: Aesthetic and operational in most exam contexts; manganese can also have health advisory considerations that require current-source verification.

# Section 7 - Disinfection Byproducts

### Card 61
Front: What are DBPs?
Back: Disinfection Byproducts.

### Card 62
Front: What causes DBPs?
Back: Disinfectants reacting with natural organic matter or other precursors.

### Card 63
Front: TTHM MCL?
Back: 80 µg/L. Source: [[Water Operator Source Bibliography#EPA-NPDWR]].

### Card 64
Front: HAA5 MCL?
Back: 60 µg/L. Source: [[Water Operator Source Bibliography#EPA-NPDWR]].

### Card 65
Front: What does TTHM stand for?
Back: Total Trihalomethanes.

### Card 66
Front: What does HAA5 stand for?
Back: Five regulated Haloacetic Acids.

### Card 67
Front: Common TTHM control method?
Back: Remove organic precursors before disinfection and manage water age/disinfectant strategy.

### Card 68
Front: What increases DBP formation?
Back: Organic precursors, disinfectant exposure, temperature, pH, and water age.

### Card 69
Front: What treatment process often reduces DBP precursors?
Back: Enhanced coagulation.

### Card 70
Front: Why are DBPs regulated?
Back: Long-term health risks.

# Section 9 - PFAS and Emerging Contaminants

### Card 81
Front: What does PFAS stand for?
Back: Per- and Polyfluoroalkyl Substances.

### Card 82
Front: Why are PFAS called forever chemicals?
Back: They resist environmental breakdown.

### Card 83
Front: PFOA MCL?
Back: 4 ppt / 4 ng/L under EPA's 2024 final PFAS NPDWR. Recheck before exam day because EPA announced proposed PFAS rule changes in 2026. Source: [[Water Operator Source Bibliography#EPA-PFAS-NPDWR]].

### Card 84
Front: PFOS MCL?
Back: 4 ppt / 4 ng/L under EPA's 2024 final PFAS NPDWR. Recheck before exam day because EPA announced proposed PFAS rule changes in 2026. Source: [[Water Operator Source Bibliography#EPA-PFAS-NPDWR]].

### Card 85
Front: Common PFAS treatment?
Back: GAC, ion exchange, RO/NF, source substitution, or blending/treatment strategy depending on compound and system design.

### Card 86
Front: What are microplastics?
Back: Small plastic particles.

### Card 87
Front: Do microplastics currently have a federal MCL?
Back: No. This remains an emerging contaminant issue rather than a standard federal MCL compliance item.

### Card 88
Front: Why are microplastics important?
Back: Active area of environmental occurrence, methods, exposure, and health-effects research.

### Card 89
Front: Common PFAS source?
Back: Firefighting foam, industrial sources, stain/water/grease-resistant products, and contaminated source water.

### Card 90
Front: Why should operators follow PFAS updates?
Back: Regulations are rapidly evolving, including EPA 2026 proposed PFAS changes and California DDW notification/response-level updates.

# Section 10 - Cyanotoxins and Source Water Risks

### Card 91
Front: What produces cyanotoxins?
Back: Cyanobacteria / blue-green algae.

### Card 92
Front: Cyanotoxins are associated with?
Back: Harmful algal blooms.

### Card 93
Front: Common cyanotoxin example?
Back: Microcystin.

### Card 94
Front: Common source-water concern during warm weather?
Back: Algal blooms.

### Card 95
Front: Why are blooms concerning?
Back: Taste, odor, filter loading, and possible toxin production.

### Card 96
Front: What is geosmin?
Back: A common earthy taste-and-odor compound.

### Card 97
Front: What is MIB?
Back: 2-Methylisoborneol, a taste-and-odor compound.

### Card 98
Front: Common taste-and-odor treatment?
Back: Activated carbon, oxidation strategy, biological treatment, or source management depending on plant design.

### Card 99
Front: What environmental factors encourage blooms?
Back: Warm temperatures, nutrients, light, and stagnant/stratified water.

### Card 100
Front: Why monitor source water?
Back: Early warning of treatment challenges.
