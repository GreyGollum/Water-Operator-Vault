# Section - Coagulation

### Card 1
Front: What is coagulation?
Back: The chemical destabilization of suspended and colloidal particles so they can clump together.

### Card 2
Front: What is the main purpose of coagulation?
Back: To make small particles easier to remove by flocculation, sedimentation, and filtration.

### Card 3
Front: What is a colloid?
Back: A very small suspended particle that does not settle easily by gravity.

### Card 4
Front: Why do colloids resist settling?
Back: They often have surface charges that repel each other and keep them dispersed.

### Card 5
Front: What does charge neutralization do?
Back: Reduces particle repulsion so particles can come together.

### Card 6
Front: What is sweep floc?
Back: A mechanism where metal hydroxide precipitate forms and enmeshes particles.

### Card 7
Front: Common aluminum-based coagulant?
Back: Alum, also called aluminum sulfate.

### Card 8
Front: Common iron-based coagulants?
Back: Ferric chloride and ferric sulfate.

### Card 9
Front: What is PACl?
Back: Polyaluminum chloride, a pre-hydrolyzed aluminum coagulant.

### Card 10
Front: What is a coagulant aid?
Back: A chemical, often polymer, used to improve floc formation or settling.

### Card 11
Front: Why does pH matter in coagulation?
Back: Coagulants work best within certain pH ranges and can change water chemistry.

### Card 12
Front: How can alum affect alkalinity?
Back: Alum consumes alkalinity and can lower pH.

### Card 13
Front: What happens if alkalinity is too low during coagulation?
Back: pH can drop and coagulation performance can suffer.

### Card 14
Front: What chemical can be used to add alkalinity?
Back: Lime, caustic soda, soda ash, or sodium bicarbonate depending on system design.

### Card 15
Front: What is enhanced coagulation?
Back: Optimizing coagulation to remove more natural organic matter and DBP precursors.

### Card 16
Front: What is TOC?
Back: Total Organic Carbon, a measure of organic material in water.

### Card 17
Front: Why remove TOC before disinfection?
Back: TOC can react with disinfectants to form DBPs.

### Card 18
Front: What is a jar test?
Back: A bench-scale test used to evaluate coagulant dose, pH, mixing, floc formation, and settling.

### Card 19
Front: What should be observed during a jar test?
Back: Floc formation, settling rate, final turbidity, pH, and treatment goals.

### Card 20
Front: What does poor pin floc indicate?
Back: Possible underdosing, wrong pH, poor mixing, or source water change.

### Card 21
Front: What can coagulant overdose cause?
Back: Poor turbidity removal, pH depression, residual metals, filter problems, and excess sludge.

### Card 22
Front: What can coagulant underdose cause?
Back: Poor particle destabilization and high settled or filtered turbidity.

### Card 23
Front: What is rapid mix?
Back: High-energy mixing to disperse coagulant quickly through raw water.

### Card 24
Front: Why must rapid mix be fast?
Back: Coagulant reactions occur quickly and need immediate dispersion.

### Card 25
Front: A sudden raw water turbidity increase should trigger what?
Back: Jar testing and process evaluation.

### Card 26
Front: What source change may increase coagulant demand?
Back: Storm runoff, algae, higher TOC, color, or turbidity.

### Card 27
Front: Why is coagulation a key barrier?
Back: It improves downstream removal of particles and pathogens.

### Card 28
Front: What does zeta potential relate to?
Back: Particle surface charge and stability.

### Card 29
Front: What does optimized coagulation protect?
Back: Sedimentation, filtration, disinfection effectiveness, and finished water quality.

### Card 30
Front: T5 scenario: settled water turbidity rises after a storm. First review?
Back: Raw water quality, coagulant dose, pH/alkalinity, rapid mix, and jar test results.


# Section - Flocculation

### Card 31
Front: What is flocculation?
Back: Gentle mixing that brings destabilized particles together to form larger floc.

### Card 32
Front: What is the main goal of flocculation?
Back: Create strong, settleable floc without breaking it apart.

### Card 33
Front: What happens if flocculation mixing is too intense?
Back: Floc can shear apart.

### Card 34
Front: What happens if flocculation mixing is too weak?
Back: Particles may not collide enough to form good floc.

### Card 35
Front: What is detention time in flocculation?
Back: The time water spends in the flocculation basin.

### Card 36
Front: Why are tapered flocculation stages used?
Back: Mixing intensity is reduced as floc gets larger and more fragile.

### Card 37
Front: What does good floc look like?
Back: Visible, well-formed, settleable particles.

### Card 38
Front: What does tiny weak floc suggest?
Back: Coagulant/pH problem or inadequate flocculation conditions.

### Card 39
Front: Why does short-circuiting hurt flocculation?
Back: Water bypasses intended mixing time.

### Card 40
Front: What equipment is used for flocculation?
Back: Paddle mixers, vertical shaft mixers, or hydraulic flocculators.

### Card 41
Front: What is the relationship between flocculation and sedimentation?
Back: Better floc improves settling.

### Card 42
Front: What is the relationship between flocculation and filtration?
Back: Better floc reduces filter loading.

### Card 43
Front: What process comes before flocculation?
Back: Coagulation/rapid mix.

### Card 44
Front: What process commonly follows flocculation?
Back: Sedimentation or clarification.

### Card 45
Front: T5 scenario: floc forms then disappears. Likely cause?
Back: Excessive mixing, poor chemistry, or fragile floc.

### Card 46
Front: T5 scenario: no visible floc in jar test. First checks?
Back: Coagulant dose, pH, alkalinity, chemical strength, and rapid mix.

### Card 47
Front: Why are flocculators divided into compartments?
Back: To control mixing intensity and contact time.

### Card 48
Front: What is floc carryover?
Back: Floc leaving the basin and loading filters.

### Card 49
Front: Why is floc carryover bad?
Back: It shortens filter runs and can increase turbidity.

### Card 50
Front: What field observation helps judge flocculation?
Back: Floc size, density, settling behavior, and settled turbidity.


# Section - Sedimentation and Clarification

### Card 51
Front: What is sedimentation?
Back: Removal of particles by gravity settling.

### Card 52
Front: What is clarification?
Back: A treatment step that removes floc and solids from water before filtration.

### Card 53
Front: What does a sedimentation basin reduce?
Back: Particle and floc loading on filters.

### Card 54
Front: What is surface overflow rate?
Back: Flow divided by basin surface area.

### Card 55
Front: What unit is common for surface overflow rate?
Back: gpd/ft².

### Card 56
Front: What is weir loading rate?
Back: Flow divided by length of outlet weir.

### Card 57
Front: What does high weir loading cause?
Back: Poor solids removal and floc carryover.

### Card 58
Front: What is sludge blanket control?
Back: Managing accumulated solids so they do not wash out.

### Card 59
Front: Why remove sludge regularly?
Back: To prevent carryover, odors, and reduced basin performance.

### Card 60
Front: What is short-circuiting in a clarifier?
Back: Water taking a shortcut through the basin, reducing effective detention.

### Card 61
Front: What do baffles do?
Back: Improve flow distribution and reduce short-circuiting.

### Card 62
Front: What are tube settlers?
Back: Inclined tubes that increase effective settling area.

### Card 63
Front: What are plate settlers?
Back: Inclined plates that improve settling efficiency.

### Card 64
Front: What does rising sludge indicate?
Back: Gas formation, excessive sludge age, or operational problems.

### Card 65
Front: What can cause poor settling?
Back: Poor coagulation, weak floc, high flow, short-circuiting, or density currents.

### Card 66
Front: What is a density current?
Back: Flow caused by temperature or solids-density differences.

### Card 67
Front: Why is settled water turbidity important?
Back: It shows how well clarification is protecting filters.

### Card 68
Front: What should happen if settled turbidity worsens?
Back: Review coagulation, flocculation, basin loading, and sludge removal.

### Card 69
Front: What is a clarifier launders?
Back: Collection troughs that receive clarified water.

### Card 70
Front: What does uneven launder flow suggest?
Back: Hydraulic imbalance.

### Card 71
Front: What is floc blanket clarification?
Back: A process that uses a suspended blanket of floc to capture particles.

### Card 72
Front: What is dissolved air flotation useful for?
Back: Removing low-density particles like algae.

### Card 73
Front: Why are algae sometimes difficult to settle?
Back: They can be buoyant and resist gravity settling.

### Card 74
Front: What does high sludge level risk?
Back: Solids carryover into filters.

### Card 75
Front: T5 scenario: filters clog quickly but raw turbidity is stable. Check?
Back: Settled water quality, sludge removal, floc carryover, and filter condition.


# Section - Filtration

### Card 76
Front: What is filtration?
Back: Passing water through media or membranes to remove particles.

### Card 77
Front: What is rapid sand filtration?
Back: A common gravity filtration process using sand or multimedia.

### Card 78
Front: What is dual-media filtration?
Back: A filter with two media layers, commonly anthracite and sand.

### Card 79
Front: What is multimedia filtration?
Back: A filter with multiple media layers such as anthracite, sand, and garnet.

### Card 80
Front: Why use anthracite over sand?
Back: It allows deeper penetration of solids and longer filter runs.

### Card 81
Front: What is filter loading rate?
Back: Flow per unit filter area, often gpm/ft².

### Card 82
Front: What is headloss?
Back: Resistance to flow through a filter as solids accumulate.

### Card 83
Front: What happens to headloss during a filter run?
Back: It generally increases.

### Card 84
Front: What is filter breakthrough?
Back: When particles pass through as filter performance worsens.

### Card 85
Front: What is filter ripening?
Back: The initial period after backwash before optimal filtration resumes.

### Card 86
Front: Why use filter-to-waste?
Back: To keep poor ripening water out of finished water.

### Card 87
Front: What is backwashing?
Back: Reversing flow to clean filter media.

### Card 88
Front: What is the purpose of backwashing?
Back: Remove accumulated solids and restore filter capacity.

### Card 89
Front: What is surface wash?
Back: Auxiliary cleaning of top media to break up solids and mudballs.

### Card 90
Front: What are mudballs?
Back: Clumps of solids and media that impair filter performance.

### Card 91
Front: What causes mudballs?
Back: Inadequate backwash or poor surface wash.

### Card 92
Front: What is air binding?
Back: Air trapped in filter media reducing flow and performance.

### Card 93
Front: What can cause air binding?
Back: Negative pressure, gas release, or improper operation.

### Card 94
Front: What is media loss?
Back: Filter media washed out during backwash or operation.

### Card 95
Front: Why monitor filter effluent turbidity?
Back: It is a critical treatment performance indicator.

### Card 96
Front: What does a sudden turbidity spike suggest?
Back: Breakthrough, media disturbance, valve issue, or process upset.

### Card 97
Front: What does steadily shortening filter runs suggest?
Back: More solids loading, poor coagulation, media fouling, or backwash problems.

### Card 98
Front: What is a filter profile?
Back: Tracking filter performance over a run.

### Card 99
Front: What is a unit filter run volume?
Back: Volume filtered during a run before backwash.

### Card 100
Front: What is terminal headloss?
Back: The headloss limit at which a filter run ends.

### Card 101
Front: What is a filter effluent particle count used for?
Back: Early warning of particle removal changes.

### Card 102
Front: What does filtered water turbidity relate to?
Back: Particle/pathogen removal performance.

### Card 103
Front: What organism is especially chlorine-resistant?
Back: Cryptosporidium.

### Card 104
Front: Why is filtration important for Cryptosporidium control?
Back: Crypto is highly resistant to normal chlorination and must be physically removed.

### Card 105
Front: What is slow sand filtration?
Back: A low-rate filtration process relying on a biological layer and fine sand.

### Card 106
Front: What is the schmutzdecke?
Back: The biologically active layer in slow sand filtration.

### Card 107
Front: What is direct filtration?
Back: Coagulation and filtration without sedimentation.

### Card 108
Front: What makes direct filtration vulnerable?
Back: It has less solids-removal buffer before filters.

### Card 109
Front: What is declining-rate filtration?
Back: A filtration mode where filter flow declines as headloss rises.

### Card 110
Front: What is constant-rate filtration?
Back: Flow is controlled to remain steady through a filter.

### Card 111
Front: What should be checked after backwash problems?
Back: Backwash rate, duration, air scour, surface wash, media expansion, and valves.

### Card 112
Front: What is media stratification?
Back: Layering of media by size/density after backwash.

### Card 113
Front: Why is underdrain integrity important?
Back: It supports media and distributes backwash/filter flow.

### Card 114
Front: T5 scenario: turbidity spikes after every backwash. Likely review?
Back: Ripening procedure, filter-to-waste, backwash rate, media, and underdrain.

### Card 115
Front: T5 scenario: one filter performs worse than others. First compare?
Back: Backwash history, media condition, valves, rate of flow, and turbidity trends.

### Card 126

### Card 137
Front: What does PAC stand for?
Back: Powdered Activated Carbon.

### Card 138
Front: How does activated carbon work?
Back: Adsorption onto high-surface-area carbon.

### Card 139
Front: What contaminants can GAC help remove?
Back: Taste/odor compounds, organics, some pesticides, and PFAS depending on design.

### Card 140
Front: What is adsorption?
Back: Attachment of molecules to a surface.

### Card 141

Back: Uptake into a material's bulk phase.

### Card 142

Back: When contaminants begin passing through the carbon bed.

### Card 143

Back: Influent concentration, empty bed contact time, media age, competition, and water quality.

### Card 144
Front: What is EBCT?
Back: Empty Bed Contact Time.

### Card 145

Back: It describes how long water contacts the carbon bed.

### Card 146

### Card 147

Back: Time or volume before media is exhausted.

### Card 148
Front: How is exhausted GAC handled?
Back: Replacement, reactivation, or disposal depending on contaminant and regulations.

### Card 149

Back: GAC supporting microbial activity to biodegrade some organics.

### Card 150
Front: Why is GAC used for taste and odor?
Back: It adsorbs compounds such as geosmin and MIB.

### Card 151
Front: Why is GAC used for PFAS?
Back: It can adsorb many PFAS, especially longer-chain compounds.

### Card 152
Front: What PFAS are often harder for GAC?

### Card 153
Front: T5 scenario: PFAS appears in GAC effluent. Meaning?
Back: Possible breakthrough and need for treatment evaluation.

### Card 154
Front: Why use lead-lag GAC vessels?
Back: To improve removal and monitor breakthrough before final effluent.

### Card 155
Front: What does lead-lag mean?
Back: Two vessels in series: lead vessel loads first; lag vessel polishes.
