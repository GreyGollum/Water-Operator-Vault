# Section 1 - Disinfection Fundamentals

### Card 1
Front: What is disinfection?
Back: Treatment used to inactivate disease-causing organisms in water.

### Card 2
Front: What is the primary purpose of disinfection?
Back: Protect public health from microbial disease.

### Card 3
Front: What is inactivation?
Back: Rendering a pathogen unable to infect or reproduce.

### Card 4
Front: What is removal?
Back: Physically taking organisms or particles out of the water.

### Card 5
Front: Why use both filtration and disinfection?
Back: Multiple barriers provide stronger public-health protection.

### Card 6
Front: What is the multiple-barrier concept?
Back: Using source protection, treatment, disinfection, storage, and distribution control together.

### Card 7
Front: Common drinking-water pathogen categories?
Back: Viruses, bacteria, Giardia, and Cryptosporidium.

### Card 8
Front: Which pathogen is especially resistant to normal chlorination?
Back: Cryptosporidium.

### Card 9
Front: Why is filtration critical for Cryptosporidium?
Back: Crypto is highly chlorine-resistant and is mainly controlled by physical removal or other validated barriers.

### Card 10
Front: What organism is commonly used in CT discussions for protozoan inactivation?
Back: Giardia.

### Card 11
Front: What does log inactivation describe?
Back: Reduction of viable organisms by powers of ten.

### Card 12
Front: 1-log inactivation equals what percent reduction?
Back: 90%.

### Card 13
Front: 2-log inactivation equals what percent reduction?
Back: 99%.

### Card 14
Front: 3-log inactivation equals what percent reduction?
Back: 99.9%.

### Card 15
Front: 4-log inactivation equals what percent reduction?
Back: 99.99%.

### Card 16
Front: What does CT stand for?
Back: Concentration × Time.

### Card 17
Front: What does CT measure?
Back: Disinfection exposure.

### Card 18
Front: What is disinfectant residual?
Back: Disinfectant remaining after demand has been satisfied.

### Card 19
Front: Why maintain residual in distribution?
Back: To help protect against microbial regrowth or intrusion.

### Card 20
Front: What is chlorine demand?
Back: Chlorine consumed by reactions before a residual remains.

# Section 2 - Chlorine Chemistry

### Card 21
Front: Chlorine dose minus chlorine residual equals what?
Back: Chlorine demand.

### Card 22
Front: What can increase chlorine demand?
Back: Organics, ammonia, iron, manganese, nitrite, sulfide, and microbial activity.

### Card 23
Front: What is contact time?
Back: Time water remains in contact with disinfectant before the compliance point.

### Card 24
Front: What is effective contact time?
Back: Contact time corrected for short-circuiting and hydraulic behavior.

### Card 25
Front: Why is disinfection not a substitute for filtration?
Back: Some pathogens and particles are best controlled by physical removal.

### Card 26
Front: What is free chlorine?
Back: Chlorine present mainly as hypochlorous acid and hypochlorite ion.

### Card 27
Front: What is hypochlorous acid?
Back: HOCl, the stronger free-chlorine disinfecting form.

### Card 28
Front: What is hypochlorite ion?
Back: OCl-, the weaker free-chlorine disinfecting form.

### Card 29
Front: Lower pH shifts free chlorine toward what form?
Back: Hypochlorous acid.

### Card 30
Front: Higher pH shifts free chlorine toward what form?
Back: Hypochlorite ion.

### Card 31
Front: Why does high pH reduce free chlorine effectiveness?
Back: More free chlorine exists as less-effective hypochlorite ion.

### Card 32
Front: Why does temperature matter for disinfection?
Back: Disinfection reactions are generally slower in colder water.

### Card 33
Front: What is sodium hypochlorite?
Back: A liquid chlorine solution commonly used for chlorination.

### Card 34
Front: What can reduce sodium hypochlorite strength over time?
Back: Heat, sunlight, age, and storage conditions.

### Card 35
Front: Why verify chemical strength?
Back: Feed settings depend on actual available chlorine concentration.

### Card 36
Front: What is breakpoint chlorination?
Back: Chlorine addition through ammonia/demand reactions until free residual appears.

### Card 37
Front: What happens before breakpoint?
Back: Chlorine reacts with ammonia and other demand substances.

### Card 38
Front: What happens after breakpoint?
Back: Free chlorine residual begins to increase.

### Card 39
Front: What is combined chlorine?
Back: Chlorine combined with ammonia or nitrogen compounds, mainly chloramines.

### Card 40
Front: What is total chlorine?
Back: Free chlorine plus combined chlorine.

### Card 41
Front: How do you calculate combined chlorine?
Back: Total chlorine minus free chlorine.

### Card 42
Front: Which is generally stronger for primary disinfection: free chlorine or chloramine?
Back: Free chlorine.

### Card 43
Front: Which residual is generally longer-lasting in distribution?
Back: Chloramine.

### Card 44
Front: What can nitrite do to residual?
Back: Consume disinfectant residual.

### Card 45
Front: What can iron and manganese do to chlorine?
Back: Increase chlorine demand through oxidation reactions.

### Card 54

Back: A test measuring actual chemical feed pump output.

### Card 55
Front: Why are residual setpoints important?
Back: They maintain disinfection while avoiding overfeed or underfeed.

### Card 56
Front: What can overchlorination increase?
Back: DBP formation, taste/odor complaints, and chemical cost.

### Card 57
Front: What can underchlorination risk?
Back: Inadequate disinfection and low distribution residual.

### Card 58
Front: Why should dose changes be controlled?
Back: Large sudden changes can create instability or compliance problems.

### Card 59

Back: Feed rate adjusts with water flow.

### Card 60
Front: What is residual-paced control?
Back: Feed rate adjusts based on measured residual.

### Card 61
Front: What is compound-loop control?
Back: Flow pacing plus residual feedback.

### Card 62
Front: Why verify online residual with grab samples?
Back: Analyzer errors can mislead operations.

### Card 63
Front: What should chlorine analyzer maintenance include?
Back: Cleaning, calibration/verification, reagents, tubing, sample flow, and records.

### Card 64
Front: What can low sample flow to an analyzer cause?
Back: False or delayed readings.

### Card 65
Front: Best response to conflicting residual readings?
Back: Verify with independent testing and check analyzer condition.

# Section 4 - CT Calculations and Contact Time

### Card 66
Front: Basic CT formula?
Back: CT = disinfectant residual × effective contact time. Source: [[Water Operator Source Bibliography#CA-EXAM-CONVERSION-2026]].

### Card 67
Front: CT units are commonly expressed as?
Back: mg-min/L.

### Card 68
Front: If residual is 1.5 mg/L and time is 30 min, CT is?
Back: 45 mg-min/L.

### Card 69
Front: If residual is 2.0 mg/L and time is 40 min, CT is?
Back: 80 mg-min/L.

### Card 70
Front: If required CT is 60 and time is 30 min, required residual is?
Back: 2.0 mg/L.

### Card 71
Front: If required CT is 90 and residual is 3.0 mg/L, required time is?
Back: 30 minutes.

### Card 72
Front: What is T10?
Back: Effective contact time often based on tracer behavior; actual method must follow the applicable guidance/table approach.

### Card 73
Front: Why use effective time instead of theoretical time?
Back: It better accounts for short-circuiting.

### Card 74
Front: What is theoretical detention time?
Back: Volume divided by flow.

### Card 75
Front: What is baffling factor?
Back: Factor used to estimate effective contact time from theoretical detention time.

### Card 76
Front: Effective contact time formula using baffling factor?
Back: T10 = theoretical detention time × baffling factor.

### Card 77
Front: A 100-minute theoretical time and 0.5 baffling factor gives T10?
Back: 50 minutes.

### Card 78
Front: Higher flow through the same basin does what to detention time?
Back: Decreases detention time.

### Card 79
Front: Lower clearwell level usually does what to contact time?
Back: Reduces available contact volume and time.

### Card 80
Front: What information is needed to evaluate CT?
Back: Residual, effective contact time, pH, temperature, disinfectant type, and organism target.

### Card 81
Front: Why do pH and temperature matter for CT tables?
Back: They affect disinfectant effectiveness.

### Card 82
Front: What happens to chlorine CT requirement in colder water?
Back: Required CT generally increases.

### Card 83
Front: What happens to free chlorine CT requirement at higher pH?
Back: Required CT generally increases.

### Card 84
Front: Actual CT must be what compared to required CT?
Back: Equal to or greater than required CT.

### Card 85
Front: Actual CT 80, required CT 70. Status?
Back: Passing.

### Card 86
Front: Actual CT 55, required CT 70. Status?
Back: Failing.

### Card 87
Front: Best first response to apparent CT failure?
Back: Verify data, protect finished water, adjust operations, and notify/escalate as required.

### Card 88
Front: What operational changes can improve CT?
Back: Increase effective time, increase residual if allowed, reduce flow, improve baffling, or increase contact volume.

### Card 89
Front: What can reduce CT margin during peak demand?
Back: Higher flow and lower storage/contact time.

### Card 90
Front: CT is close to failing during high flow. What should be reviewed?
Back: Flow, clearwell level, T10, residual, pH, temperature, and operating constraints.

# Section 6 - Chloramines

### Card 111
Front: What are chloramines?
Back: Combined chlorine compounds formed by chlorine reacting with ammonia.

### Card 112
Front: Main chloramine used for drinking-water residual?
Back: Monochloramine.

### Card 113
Front: Why use chloramines?
Back: Longer-lasting distribution residual and often lower TTHM formation.

### Card 114
Front: Disadvantage of chloramines?
Back: Weaker disinfectant and nitrification risk.

### Card 115
Front: What is the chlorine-to-ammonia ratio important for?
Back: Forming stable monochloramine.

### Card 116
Front: What does too much free ammonia risk?
Back: Nitrification.

### Card 117
Front: What is nitrification?
Back: Microbial conversion of ammonia to nitrite/nitrate in chloraminated systems.

### Card 118
Front: Signs of nitrification?
Back: Residual loss, rising nitrite/nitrate, pH changes, microbial indicators, and taste/odor issues.

### Card 119
Front: Why does nitrite matter in chloraminated systems?
Back: Nitrite consumes disinfectant residual.

### Card 120
Front: Nitrification response may include what?
Back: Monitoring, flushing, residual/ammonia control, tank turnover, and regulator-approved actions.

### Card 121
Front: Temporary free-chlorine conversion may be used for what?
Back: Controlling nitrification and biofilm in chloraminated systems, if approved and planned.

### Card 122
Front: What must be considered before disinfectant conversion?
Back: Regulatory approval/notification, DBPs, customers, corrosion, and distribution hydraulics.

### Card 123
Front: Total chlorine in chloraminated water is mostly what?
Back: Combined chlorine residual.

### Card 124
Front: Why monitor free ammonia?
Back: It helps detect nitrification risk and chloramine formation problems.

### Card 125
Front: Why monitor nitrite?
Back: It can indicate nitrification and has regulatory significance.

### Card 126
Front: Why monitor pH in chloramination?
Back: pH affects chloramine formation and stability.

### Card 127
Front: How does water age affect chloramines?
Back: Higher water age can lead to residual decay and nitrification.

### Card 128
Front: Low residual and high water age suggest what?
Back: Evaluate storage turnover, flushing, and disinfectant strategy.

### Card 129
Front: Total chlorine drops, nitrite rises, free ammonia present. Likely issue?
Back: Nitrification.

### Card 130
Front: Best chloramine-control mindset?
Back: Balance residual stability, nitrification prevention, DBP control, and corrosion impacts.

Back: It promotes residual loss and water quality deterioration.

Back: It can create poor mixing, dead zones, and residual decay.

Back: Water age increases and residual can decay.
